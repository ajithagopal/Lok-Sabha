18th Lok Sabha 2024 - Present

The Modi mandate weakens

The era of coalition governance returns as the BJP-led NDA's electoral pitch of welfarism, Article 370's abrogation and the Ram Temple is drowned out by concerns of unemployment, inflation and democratic erosion.

2024 - Present
Bharatiya Janata Party
Narendra Modi
June 5, 2024 - Present
Rahul Gandhi
Om Birla

The front page gives it away. The Hindu’s headline five years ago confidently read ‘India Gives Modi a High Five.’ In 2024, the hand wavered, as the title chosen is “BJP falls short, need allies to govern.” On the mighty inked finger is a caricature of Prime Minister Narendra Modi perched next to Opposition leader Rahul Gandhi — a picture that defines the politics of the 18th Lok Sabha. An unsteady dependence overtook BJP’s unassailable dominance in the previous two elections. The BJP government’s mandate was weaker; meanwhile, the Opposition INDIA bloc defied expectations and regional parties thrived. On June 5, the Indian electorate map was less saffron, more colourful, with shades of green, red and blue. It was a pyrrhic victory for the BJP.

The Lower House formed at the tail end of a long-winded election was unlike its nearest predecessors. Its agenda was almost a decade in the making.

Unemployment, agrarian distress and inflation had peaked, personal and political freedoms had declined. Extreme weather and artificial intelligence posed new challenges. Against this, the ruling government counted as wins its GDP-led growth, welfare measures, Ram Temple’s construction and Article 370’s abrogation. It cited India’s rise as a global power and the fastest growing economy, countering China’s influence on the global stage.

But its pitch faltered in the lead-up to the 18th Lok Sabha. The 2024 verdict showed the “limits of communal polarisation” while revealing a desire for constructive nation building, The Hindu’s editorial noted. “The BJP must heed that message in a democratic spirit, and reorient itself to the reality of the reemergence of the coalition politics after 10 years.”

The Hindu's editorial published on June 5, 2024

The polls

India conducted the world’s largest election exercise between April 19 and June 1, spread across seven-phases. 64.2 crore voters, accounting for 10% of the world’s population, elected members to Lok Sabha’s 543 seats. Over 8,000 candidates were in the fray, with 16% fielded by national parties, 6% by State parties, and 47% of candidates being independents, a report by PRS Legislative Research said. This is the highest number of candidates since 1996.

Heatwave conditions ahead of the elections prompted concerns about voter safety and low turnout, and measures were stepped up. The turnout was measured at 65.79%, as per the Election Commission data. The Hindu in its editorial published on May 29 noted “a significant dampening in turnout in this set of elections in comparison to the 2019 and even the 2014 general elections.”

An analysis of ECI’s data finds that in the first five phases, turnout fell in northern states (such as Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan and Uttar Pradesh), and increased in southern and western regions such as Karnataka, Telangana and Maharashtra. Until May 24, Nagaland registered the biggest dip in voter turnout, from 82.91% in 2019 to 57.72% this year. The Eastern Naga People’s Organisation had urged the constituency’s four lakh voters to abstain from voting over its longstanding demand for a separate state comprising the six districts. Elsewhere, such as in Kerala, Mumbai and Lucknow, voter apathy gutted people’s participation too.

In the final count, Lakshadweep recorded the highest voter turnout at 84% followed by Assam at 81%. The lowest turnout was recorded in Bihar at 56.19%. Uttar Pradesh (56.92%) and Mizoram (56.87%) were the other low turnout states.

A marked change emerged in Kashmir which headed to polls after years of boycotting the Indian Elections. Encouraging was the turnout in Srinagar and Baramulla; the latter registered its highest voter turnout since 1984. Livelihood concerns, internet censorship and “the limitations on political activity in the Valley since 2018 have constrained citizens who have had little recourse to airing their grievances due to the absence of legislative representation in the Assembly”,

The Hindu noted in its editorial. The greater electoral participation reflected the need for representation of the electorate’s voices, The Hindu noted while warning that the BJP “should not misinterpret the increase” as people look at the ballot box as a tool to break the political morass.Summer elections were, once upon a time, unthinkable in India. But precedent changed post 2004. This year, the 44-day election cycle overlapped with an acute heatwave, which the National Disaster Management Authority warned could expose people to health hazards. Heat spells cast a sticky shadow over both campaigning and polling; the ECI suspected ambient heat dissuaded voters in the early phases of the General Election.

The BJP huffed and puffed its way across the halfway mark, falling almost 32 short of an absolute majority (it had secured 303 parliamentary seats in 2019). The Congress, on the other hand, won 99 seats in contrast to its 52 seats in 2019.

The NDA secured 291 seats against the Congress-led INDIA bloc’s 234 seats.

The Hindu’s editorial remarked, “People are, and should be, at the centre of a democracy. The outcome of the 18th general election is an unambiguous reiteration by the people of that tenet.”

Political scientist Ashwani Kumar of the Tata Institute of Social Sciences (TISS) looked at the results as a return to a more centrist political outlook. “Given the rich tradition of centrist politics in India, the BJP is set to enter into the Rajdharma of rainbow coalition, reverting to Atal Behari Vajpayee’s consensual politics of governance [while] reinventing the idea of cooperative federalism. Thus, let’s expect what a legendary political scientist called a ‘consocial’ or power sharing democracy, within the majoritarian polity of India,” he said.

The Lok Sabha elections also saw simultaneous Assembly elections for Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh, Andhra Pradesh and Odisha.

To beat the heat, a YSRCP supporter covers his face with the party flag during the nomination rally of YSRCP South MLA candidate Vasupalli Ganesh Kumar in Visakhapatnam on April 20, 2024. Photo: V. Raju/The Hindu

The house of the people

The BJP faced setbacks in its trusted bastions while making headway elsewhere. It registered gains in Odisha, winning 19 of the 21 Lok Sabha seats. The party drew a blank in Tamil Nadu but its vote share increased to double digits, without forming an alliance either with the DMK or AIADMK. The party also made a breakthrough in Kerala, with actor-turned-politician Suresh Gopi winning from the Thrissur constituency. Its alliance with Janata Dal (Secular) handed the party 19 seats in Karnataka, with the Congress winning nine. In Telangana, both BJP and Congress won eight seats each, wiping out the regional Bharat Rashtra Samiti (BRS).

Mr. Modi relied on his individual charm and magnetism as a populist leader but the Modi wave wasn’t as pronounced as the previous elections. Mr. Modi’s aura, albeit alluring, is also “inherently unstable, and as power is now so concentrated in him, it can even damage his own party’s prospects in the long run,” noted Radhika Santhanam in The Hindu.

This is the first election with INDIA bloc, who came out shining. “As the going gets tough for NDA, INDIA gets going”, stated The Hindu in its breakdown of the votes.

Verdict 2024: The Hindu's double page spread on the Lok Sabha results on June 5, 2024.

INDIA witnessed a comeback in UP with its ally Samajwadi Party registering a resurgence. It also made headways in other Hindi belt States like Rajasthan, Bihar, Haryana and Jharkhand. While Congress lost Chinchwara, a seat it held for 40 years, its strike rate improved this time from 9.3% to 28.3%, data showed.

Acting as a bulwark against BJP were regional parties. The BJP ended with lower tallies in Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan. Victories in some parts echoed people’s anxieties over jobs, reservation and Constitutional integrity. Although the consecration of the idol in Ayodhya was expected to be a win for the BJP, two holy sites for Ram rejected the BJP: Samajwadi Party’s Dalit candidate Awadesh Prasad won from Faizabad (home to Ayodhya) and DMK alliance partner IUML's Navaskani, won from Ramanathapuram (home to Rameshwaram). The BJP lost Ayodhya and Muzaffarnagar, the citadels of Hindu assertion.

Its controversial Citizenship Amendment Act, implemented weeks before the West Bengal elections, failed to make a dent in the State. In other battleground states like Maharashtra, the split of the two major regional parties, the Shiv Sena and National Congress Party, divided votes. The Mahayuti alliance — comprising of the BJP, Shiv Sena, Nationalist Congress Party and Rashtriya Samaj Paksha (RSP) – contested the Maha Vikas Aghadi– with the Indian National Congress, Shiv Sena (Uddhav Balasaheb Thackeray) (Shiv Sena-UBT), and the Nationalist Congress Party (Sharadchandra Pawar) (NCP–SP). The latter made gains.

There were multiple players of the match, including MK Stalin, Akhilesh Yadav, Mamata Banerjee, Rahul Gandhi, and the Bharat Adivasi Party’s Rajkumar Roat.

At the finishing line though, Bihar’s Nitish Kumar and Andhra Pradesh’s Chandrababu Naidu emerged as key figures, winning a crucial number of Lok Sabha seats. "Nitish and Naidu play kingmakers,” reported The Hindu’s Sobhana K. Nair, as the survival of an NDA government depended entirely on whether these two remained in the alliance and lent their seat tally, ensuring that NDA crossed the required majority mark.

The politics

Among the usual mudslinging between opponents, there was a third party that became the target of Opposition ire – the Election Commission.

Opposition parties questioned its credibility and slammed it for “failing to fulfil its constitutional duty,” particularly after there was a delay in release of turnout data, in addition to an increase in the revised turnout data released a few days after each polling phase. It also did not release absolute turnout information.

The Supreme Court also refused to accept pleas by civil society organisations that the EC release information about voters, particularly from Form 17C. Form 17C provides data about voter turnout in every polling station, and is meant to be filled up by the Presiding Officer of the polling station on the day of polling.

The Hindu noted in an editorial that the ECI had “disappointed the Indian electorate by its inability to be effective, impartial and prompt in its role as the watchdog of elections. This is partly a function of the mechanism of the appointment of the ECI’s members, which is entirely a partisan decision of the executive.”

Ahead of the elections, the Electronic Voting Machines (EVM) mechanism was also called into question. A petition by the Association of Democratic Reforms in the Supreme Court requested that there is 100% cross-verification of vote counts against VVPAT (Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail). The Supreme Court rejected this plea, but said that the burnt memory of the microcontroller in 5% of EVMs would be checked by a team of engineers after results were declared, at the written request of candidates coming in second or third. The existing practice was the cross-verification by VVPAT slips of five random EVMs in each Assembly segment/constituency.

Allegations of voter suppression also made the rounds, with Muslims alleging such suppression in some neighbourhoods of Mathura. Ahead of the elections, YSRCP filed a complaint with the ECI saying that TDP was using an app to collect voter information and profile voters in Andhra Pradesh. In several centres across the country, people discovered their names missing from the voter lists.

There were also challenges associated with the nomination of candidates. The nomination of the Samajwadi Party’s Khajuraho candidate Meera Yadav was rejected for irregularities, which the party termed as a “well-planned conspiracy.” In Indore, the Congress candidate Akshay Kanti Bam joined the BJP weeks before polling. In Varanasi, the home constituency of Prime Minister Modi, the nomination of comedian Shyam Rangeela was rejected, with the ECI saying it was “incomplete.” After the rejection of his nomination, Mr. Rangeela in a video message on X alleged that citizens were being deprived of their right to contest polls.

The lead-up to the polls saw the arrest of opposition leaders, the most prominent being that of Delhi Chief Minister Arvind Kejriwal and AAP leader Sanjay Singh in connection with the Delhi liquor excise policy scam. Mr. Singh was arrested last October and released on bail in April 2024. Mr. Kejriwal was arrested in March 2024 and released on temporary bail for 21 days on May 11; he reentered Tihar Jail on June 2. The arrest had led to a public outcry. “This is what dictatorship does, it takes the Chief Minister of a State that has come to power with a huge mandate and sends him to jail to send a message that this can happen to anyone. Without any proof, without any recovery, anyone can be jailed,” Mr. Kejriwal said.

A Supreme Court judgement declaring the electoral bonds scheme unconstitutional, and the subsequent release of data by the ECI about electoral bonds anonymously purchased by contributors and encashed by political parties between April 2019 and February 15, 2024, caused a ripple and raised concerns about transparency. Among all political parties, the Bharatiya Janata Party encashed electoral bonds worth ₹6,060.5 crore — the highest among all parties, at 47.5% of all bonds encashed. All India Trinamool Congress followed with ₹1,609.50 crore (12.6%), while third on the list was the Congress, which received ₹1,421.9 crore (11.1%).

During the campaign itself, hate speech was a key issue. The Election Commission of India (ECI) asked the BJP and the Congress to desist from raising divisive issues in campaigning in the general election. Mr. Modi had referred to Muslims as “infiltrators” and “people with more children.

In what was the first time for a sitting Prime Minister, the EC issued a notice for violation of the Model Code of Conduct based on complaints against Prime Minister Narendra Modi for his speech in Banswara, Rajasthan. Taking a circuitous route, the notice, however, was served to BJP President J.P. Nadda, and not Mr. Modi. A similar notice was served to Congress president Mallikarjun Kharge, concerning complaints related both to him and Rahul Gandhi. The EC also issued showcause notices to Congress leader Supriya Shrinate, BJP MP from West Bengal Dilip Ghosh, Aam Aadmi Party leader Atishi, Congress leader Randeep Singh Surjewala, and Bharat Rashtra Samithi chief K. Chandrashekar Rao

“The ECI is assuming a false parity between legitimate debates on policies that impact various social groups differently and an incitement of xenophobia for social polarisation,” an editorial in The Hindu noted.

Disinformation, as always, remained a concern, along with a new layer of threat generated by Artificial Intelligence (AI). Deepfakes have proliferated the web, amplifying the already prevalent threat of fake news on social media. Recent instances include deepfakes of the late M. Karunanidhi exhorting party cadre, as his son, Tamil Nadu Chief Minister M.K. Stalin, looks on; a Tamil dub of Prime Minister Narendra Modi addressing a gathering in Chennai; and videos of Madhya Pradesh leaders Shivraj Singh Chouhan and Kamal Nath with doctored remarks.

As NDA and India traded barbs, there was a surprise entrant in the battlefield: the Constitution emerged as a site of contestation, even among BJP supporters. The Congress’s Rahul Gandhi took to symbolically waving a copy of the Constitution at his campaign rallies. The fact Indians are worried about the Constitution is of far greater political significance than the question of who wins the election, wrote Yamini Aiyar and Neelanjan Sircar in The Hindu.

Concerns over electoral autocracy, free speech and the independence of the judiciary also played a role. Laws governing internet content and the creation of a central fact check unit attracted detractors. India’s standing on human rights also came under question, with its NCRB accreditation being put on hold.

“BJP sought to centralise power within itself and use the cult of Mr. Modi’s personality to undermine formal parliamentary and electoral processes…, citizens began to seek alternative sites to exert democratic pressures,” Yamini Aiyar and Nilanjan Sircar wrote in The Hindu.

The agenda

The 18th Lok Sabha started with a rarely held contest for the position of Speaker, with Om Birla finally being chosen. INDIA demanded that the Deputy Speaker position be accorded to it; the position has remained empty over the last two terms. Rahul Gandhi has taken over as Leader of Opposition, with Congress finally having enough seats in the House to qualify as the largest Opposition party with over 10% strength in the House– a prerequisite for selecting a leader of the Opposition.

Post the election of Speaker, the Lok Sabha adopted a motion decrying the emergency, thus already starting off with a chorus of protests from the Opposition.

The election of the Speaker and an address by the President took place during a special session of the Parliament from June 24 to July 3.

The first key moment during the 18th Lok Sabha was the declaration of the Union Budget- the first one for the NDA’s third term, and also Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman’s record seventh time.

Early days of Parliament have also been marred by controversy surrounding the NEET-UG entrance exam. The tragic death of three students due to flooding at a civil services exam coaching centre in Delhi, landslide in Wayanad, floods across the country, and multiple train accidents have also been the focus of demands for discussion during the first days of the Budget session.

Many more issues are set to arise, both within the house and without, that will guide the path of the 18th Lok Sabha.
